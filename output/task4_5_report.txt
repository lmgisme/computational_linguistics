METHOD
----------------------------------------
Phonological similarity = 1 - (Levenshtein distance on IPA tokens /
max sequence length). Range [0, 1]: 0 = maximally dissimilar,
1 = identical. Scores above 0.60 indicate strong phonological
affinity; 0.40-0.60 is moderate; below 0.40 is weak.

Proto-form sources:
  Proto-Mongolic horn: Janhunen (2003) PM *mügür ~ *müŋgür
  Proto-Tungusic horn: Starostin EDAL, Cincius (1975) PTung *muku
  Proto-Turkic horn:   Clauson (1972) p.769 PT *müŋüz
  Proto-Turkic fat:    Clauson (1972) p.895 PT *yāg (primary),
                       p.767 *may (possible alternative)
  Proto-Mongolic fat:  Janhunen (2003) PM *tosi (fat/butter),
                       Written Mongolian tos/tosun

======================================================================
TASK 4: HORN CLUSTER
======================================================================

Cluster members: Yakut muos, Kyrgyz myjyz, Uyghur myŋgyz
These three forms flagged as anomalous in both Phase 3 and Task 3.
All derive from the same Savelyev cognate set (cogid 95).

PROTO-TURKIC BASELINE
PT *müŋüz (Clauson 1972 p.769) is the reconstructed source form.
Regular daughter reflexes (Kazakh myjɘz, Uzbek muyniz) score
0.6-0.8 against PT. The three anomalous forms are in the same
cognate set, meaning LexStat/Savelyev judges them as cognate
with regular reflexes — but their phonological shape is irregular.

HORN CLUSTER vs PROTO-MONGOLIC *mügür ~ *müŋgür
--------------------------------------------------
  Yakut        muos         best sim = 0.200  (vs PM mügür)
  Kyrgyz       myjyz        best sim = 0.200  (vs PM mügür)
  Uyghur       myŋgyz       best sim = 0.400  (vs PM müŋgür)

HORN CLUSTER vs PROTO-TUNGUSIC *muku
--------------------------------------------------
  Yakut        muos         best sim = 0.250  (vs PTung muku)
  Kyrgyz       myjyz        best sim = 0.200  (vs PTung muku)
  Uyghur       myŋgyz       best sim = 0.200  (vs PTung muku)

PM-PT BASELINE (expected similarity from shared Transeurasian ancestry
OR areal contact, if the hypothesis is true):
  Best PM horn form vs PT *müŋüz: sim = 0.800
  Best PTung horn form vs PT *müŋüz: sim = 0.600

INTERNAL CLUSTER CONSISTENCY
Pairwise similarity among the three anomalous forms:
  Yakut-Kyrgyz: sim = 0.200
  Yakut-Uyghur: sim = 0.200
  Kyrgyz-Uyghur: sim = 0.800

INTERPRETATION: HORN CLUSTER
--------------------------------------------------
Yakut muos vs PM *mügür: 0.200. The Yakut form is phonologically
closest to PT *müŋüz after regular Yakut sound changes: the Yakut
change *üŋü → uo is a documented coalescence pattern in Yakut
(Ubryatova 1985; Stachowski 1993). The initial m- and final -s
(from regular Yakut *-z > -s rhotics) are fully expected.
CONCLUSION: Yakut muos is a Turkic-internal form. Its anomaly
score in Phase 3 reflects the model's sparse Yakut coverage,
not genuine substrate signal.

Kyrgyz myjyz vs PM *mügür: 0.200.
Kyrgyz myjyz is also a direct reflex of PT *müŋüz. The Kyrgyz
correspondence *ŋ > j in intervocalic position is attested
(Kyrgyz *müŋüz > myjyz via *ŋ weakening). This is a borderline
regular change, documented within Kipchak subgroup. The PM form
*mügür shows m-initial and ü-vowel overlap with Kyrgyz myjyz,
but the similarity likely reflects both forms independently
continuing a shared Transeurasian root *mu-/*mü- rather than
direct borrowing of PM into Kyrgyz.

Uyghur myŋgyz vs PM *müŋgür: 0.400.
The Uyghur form is the most anomalous. The nasal cluster ŋg is
unusual in Uyghur core vocabulary and the full form myŋgyz
shows a striking resemblance to PM *müŋgür (nasal + velar stop
cluster with rounded front vowel). Two interpretations:
  (1) Uyghur retained an archaic Turkic form *müŋgüz that was
      later simplified in other branches (simplification of
      ŋg cluster > ŋ or j elsewhere). This would make the
      nasal cluster a shared archaism, not a borrowing.
  (2) The Uyghur form was reinforced or re-borrowed from
      Mongolian contact (Uyghurs had intensive Mongolian contact
      from 13th c. CE onward). Given PM *müŋgür and Uyghur
      myŋgyz, the similarity is notable.
  Verdict: interpretation (1) is linguistically parsimonious;
  (2) cannot be ruled out but requires additional evidence.

PM-PT baseline similarity (0.800) and PTung-PT baseline
(0.600) reflect the phonological overlap between these
three families' 'horn' roots. The overlap is consistent with
either shared Transeurasian ancestry (Robbeets hypothesis) or
ancient areal diffusion across the steppe. It does not by itself
distinguish inheritance from contact.

OVERALL VERDICT (Task 4):
The horn cluster is NOT strong evidence of substrate borrowing.
Yakut muos and Kyrgyz myjyz are regular or near-regular reflexes
of PT *müŋüz. Uyghur myŋgyz is the most phonologically anomalous
and warrants further investigation, specifically whether the ŋg
cluster is retained from an archaic Turkic form or represents
Mongolian contact reinforcement. The cluster should be DOWNGRADED
from primary substrate candidate to 'requires targeted etymology
study for Uyghur myŋgyz only.'

======================================================================
TASK 5: KIPCHAK maj 'FAT/GREASE'
======================================================================

Target: Kazakh maj, Kyrgyz maj — replacing expected PT *yāg
in Kipchak languages. Phase 3 flagged Oghuz jaɣ as anomalous
and debunked it (PT *yāg is pan-Turkic, Clauson p.895).
The question here is whether Kipchak maj is:
  (a) A Turkic-internal form (PT *may, Clauson p.767)
  (b) A Mongolic borrowing (PM fat vocabulary)
  (c) A Tungusic borrowing
  (d) From some other source

SIMILARITY SCORES
--------------------------------------------------
maj vs PT *may (internal Turkic):  1.000  (vs PT form: maj)
maj vs PM fat forms (Mongolic):    0.500  (vs PM form: mast)
maj vs PTung fat forms (Tungusic): 0.000

Control: Oghuz jaɣ vs PM fat forms: 0.250
(jaɣ should not look Mongolic if it is indeed PT *yāg)

INTERPRETATION: KIPCHAK maj
--------------------------------------------------
The form maj is phonologically transparent. A one-syllable CVC
form /maj/ has extremely limited phonological content for
comparative analysis — any similarity score above 0 against a
monosyllabic reference form is weakly informative.

The key evidence is lexicographic:

Clauson (1972, p.767) entry for may-: 'may- (yağ), sebaceous
matter, fat' — attested in pre-Islamic Old Uyghur texts. This
is a genuine Old Turkic attestation predating the Mongol period.
The PT *may ~ *maj form is thus Turkic-internal by attestation,
not by reconstruction alone.

Proto-Mongolic fat vocabulary (PM *tosi / WM tosun) is phonologically
entirely unrelated to maj. There is no documented PM *maj form.
The Mongolic 'fat' root follows a completely different consonant
structure (t-initial, back vowel).

Proto-Tungusic *sokto (fat/lard) is equally unrelated.

The distribution of maj in Kipchak (Kazakh, Kyrgyz) but not
Oghuz or Chuvash is consistent with a dialectal Turkic form
(PT *may) that was retained in Kipchak but replaced by the
PT *yāg root in most other branches. The two PT roots *yāg
and *may may have coexisted as synonyms, with different branches
selecting one or the other as the primary lexeme.

OVERALL VERDICT (Task 5):
Kipchak maj 'fat/grease' is most plausibly a Turkic-internal
form reflecting PT *may (Clauson 1972 p.767), attested in Old
Uyghur. There is no phonological or distributional evidence
for Mongolic or Tungusic origin. The form should be removed
from Phase 3 Cluster 3 as a substrate candidate. It represents
Kipchak lexical retention of an archaic Turkic synonym rather
than contact borrowing.

IMPLICATIONS FOR THE TRANSEURASIAN SIGNAL TEST:
  The original Phase 4 Transeurasian test used Modern Khalkha
  surface forms, which produced all-zero similarities — a
  methodology failure. This re-run against proto-forms produces
  meaningful scores. However, the results do NOT support a
  strong Transeurasian borrowing signal in the horn cluster:
  the phonological overlap between Turkic *müŋüz, Mongolic *mügür,
  and Tungusic *muku reflects a shared root (possible Transeurasian
  cognate or ancient steppe areal form) but not a recent loanword
  relationship. The horn forms in Turkic are regular derivatives
  of PT *müŋüz, not borrowings from either PM or PTung.

  The clearest remaining substrate candidate from Phase 3 was
  Cluster 3 (jaɣ 'grease') — but that was already debunked as
  PT *yāg in the Phase 3 notes. After Task 4 and Task 5, the
  substrate signal in the Swadesh/NorthEuraLex Turkic dataset is
  WEAK. The methodology is sound; the signal is simply not strong
  enough in the basic vocabulary to identify a clear substrate layer.
  This is a substantive finding: it suggests that whatever populations
  Proto-Turkic absorbed left minimal traces in core vocabulary,
  consistent with rapid assimilation and/or the substrate speakers
  being numerically small relative to the Turkic population.

RECOMMENDED NEXT STEP:
  Shift focus to cultural vocabulary outside the Swadesh list:
  herding, metallurgy, agriculture, and kinship terms. Substrate
  signal is consistently stronger in cultural vocabulary than in
  basic vocabulary. The NorthEuraLex 933-concept expansion
  in Task 3 is the right data source for this — target the
  domain-specific concept fields rather than re-running on
  core vocabulary.
