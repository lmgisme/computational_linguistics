Turkic Computational Historical Linguistics Project

METHODOLOGY
--------------------------------------------------
Two complementary distance metrics compare Phase 3 substrate
candidate clusters against Mongolic (Khalkha), Tungusic (Evenki),
and Persian reference lexicons:

  A. Phonological similarity: 1 - (Levenshtein distance / max_len)
     on IPA token sequences, per word per gloss.
  B. Normalized Compression Distance (NCD): cluster vocabulary
     vs reference vocabulary, information-theoretically.
     NCD ~ 0 = high relatedness; NCD ~ 1 = unrelated.

Origin probabilities combine A and B (weight 0.6/0.4) with
an unknown residual for clusters where known-family similarity
is low. These are heuristic scores, NOT Bayesian posteriors.
They should be treated as prior-to-BEAST hypotheses.

4. INTERPRETATION
--------------------------------------------------
Cluster 3 (grease: jaɣ/jɔɣ — Azerbaijani, Turkmen, Uzbek):
  Highest P(Mongolic) in dataset. The form jaɣ maps closely to
  Mongolic 'fat/grease' semantics. Mongolic jaɣ/tos (fat) is a
  documented Turkic-Mongolic isogloss. The three-language spread
  (Oghuz + Uzbek) is consistent with a westward borrowing after
  Mongolic contact during the Mongol expansion (13th c. CE), though
  the form could also reflect Transeurasian inheritance.
  RECOMMENDATION: Primary Transeurasian candidate. Test against
  expanded Mongolic Swadesh list in Phase 5.

Cluster 2 (all: ʒɯɣɯl/ʥɯɣɯl — Kazakh, Kyrgyz, Uyghur):
  Moderate P(Mongolic). The ɣ-medial structure and front-harmonic
  vowels are consistent with Mongolic phonotactics. Chuvash χölɣa
  'ear' lands here via clustering, likely noise given its divergent
  semantics. The 'all' forms warrant direct comparison against
  Middle Mongolian bügüde/hamug forms.
  RECOMMENDATION: Secondary Transeurasian candidate. Note that
  'all' is cross-linguistically unstable in Swadesh lists and
  prone to replacement; treat with caution.

Cluster 4 (horn: muos/myjyz/myŋgyz — Yakut, Kyrgyz, Uyghur):
  Low P(Mongolic), low P(Tungusic). Tungusic 'horn' (muki) shows
  partial phonological overlap with Yakut muos (m-u initial), but
  the full correspondence is weak. The Yakut form muos likely
  reflects Proto-Turkic *müŋüz with regular Yakut vowel coalescence
  (ü+ü > uo), making it internal Turkic innovation rather than
  substrate. The Uyghur myŋgyz nasal cluster is more anomalous.
  RECOMMENDATION: Retain for Phase 5 comparison but low priority.
  Yakut muos is probably Turkic-internal; Uyghur myŋgyz may be
  a phonological variant or Mongolian contact form.

Clusters 1 and 5 (mixed: small, many, tree, bone, egg...):
  These clusters are heterogeneous — they combine Yakut innovations,
  Persian loanwords (Uzbek daraxt 'tree'), and words flagged by
  model coverage gaps rather than genuine substrate signal. P(Unknown)
  is highest here. Do NOT treat these as coherent substrate candidates.
  Individual items worth re-examining: Yakut uŋuoχ 'bone' (unusual
  V-nasal-V structure) and Yakut sɯmɯ:t 'egg' (diverges from the
  pan-Turkic jumurta/ʒumurtqa cognate set).

OVERALL TRANSEURASIAN ASSESSMENT:
  Cluster 3 provides the strongest computational signal for
  Mongolic contact in the Turkic lexicon. This is consistent with
  documented Turkic-Mongolic lexical exchange, but does not by
  itself resolve the inheritance-vs-contact debate.
  No cluster shows strong Tungusic signal. If Transeurasian
  genetic relatedness is real, the signature is either (a) too
  decayed to detect in a 40-item list, or (b) has been obscured
  by the heavy Mongolic contact layer during the medieval period.
  The 40-item Swadesh list is a limiting factor throughout:
  a 200-item list would substantially improve detection power.
